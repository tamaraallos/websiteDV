Visualising Global Mortality Causes Over Time

Viewing and interacting with sixty years of data from all around the world

Visualisation One: Combination Choropleth and Line Chart


This interactive choropleth, built using D3.js, presents global mortality rates for major causes of death using data from the OECD Health Statistics reports. The data are represented using a colour scale to quantise the relative severity of each cause across different sex, and year, as well as offering a broad, at-a-glance comparison of any regional mortality patterns.

When the user hovers over any country on the choropleth, a detailed line chart appears. This line chart illustrates mortality trends for the selected cause over time, spanning all available historical data. This interactive feature enables users to not only compare global data but also to examine how a particular cause of death has evolved in individual countries over the years. This line chart is intended to serve a similar purpose as a generic tooltip, being that it grants the viewer an additional level of information on top of the baseline choropoleth visualisation.

The choropleth is placed on a card-like element, which includes a legend to clarify the available user interactions - a set of five hotkeys is available for use, each brushing the data in a distinct manner. Users can filter data dynamically by toggling between different causes and years, enabling the chance to identify regional or global patterns. The end-user experience has had considerable thought, with ease of use and intuitive controls at front of mind. For those which suffer a colour-vision deficiency, an alternate colour palette is accessible with the f (fill) key. For those interested in scrubbing through the various causes for any particular country while viewing the full-time line chart, the ability to use the c (cause) hotkey exists. For the baseline choropleth visualisation, the user is also able to scrub through the years (forwards or backwards) with the left and right arrow keys, and is able to cycle through the available sexes with the s key.

The intent for this visualisation was to provide a broad view of global rates of death from various parent-level categories, rather than flood the viewer with over 50 distinct sub-categories; this design approach was selected to not over-clutter the data and to ensure that the viewer is able to take in as much information at a glance as possible without requiring manual thought. Ideally, the ability to view pockets of geographical trends may allow health professionals, researchers, and even policymakers to make changes to their understanding and approach towards public health at a global scale.

Visualisation Two: Stacked Area Chart

This visaulisation is of mortality data using a stacked bar chart. This is to showcase trends in deaths causes by a varity of different causes of mortality over time, grouped by decades. It allows users to interactively explore the causes of death by selecting specific gender, countries and causes, filtering the data dynamically.

The visualisation features colour-coded bars, where each cause of death is represented by a specific color, making it easier to compare. A tooltip is also added to provide further detail about specific causes and their associated death rates when users hover over the bar. There is a slight transition that also occurs when users do hover over a bar.

Lastly, a legend table is provided to provide clarity to users for what each cause colour represents as well as enabling users to toggle causes on and off. This updates the chart dynamically and shows only the selected causes. The causes filter restarts when users choose a different country/sex.

This is useful for public health professional, researchers, policymakers or people interested in mortality who want to track how mortality patterns evolve, identify patterns, prominent causes of death in different countries, or evaluate the impact of health interventions over time. By visualisaing this data in a stacked bar chart, users can gain insights into trends quickly that would be difficult to see through raw datasets.

Muzner's guidelines and Tufte's design principles were taken into consideration when developing the stacked bar chart. The chart design emphiased on accuracy, clarity and effiency in portraying the data. Munzner's guideline focused on matching visual representation to the task, using appriopiate encodings, and balancing usability and complexity. While Tufte's principles of avoiding unncessary clutter, implementing data-ink maximisation and making sure that every visual element provides a purpose in communicating data insight. As a result of these principles, they help to create an informative and inuttive visualisaiton.


Data Sources and Transformation

All data were sourced from the OECD Health Statistics datasets. The Health status dataset includes data about Causes of mortality, which formed the foundation of our final data.

The unfiltered dataset contains ~952,000 data points, and was transformed in a relatively simple manner to reduce the overall size and bloat. As introduced in previous Data Science units, KNIME was utilised to smoothly mould our data into a more desirable form.
