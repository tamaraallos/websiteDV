Visualising Global Mortality Causes Over Time

How have the proportional rates of various causes of mortality changed over the past sixty years?

Visualisation One: Combination Choropleth and Line Chart

Visualisation Two: Stacked Area Chart

This visaulisation is of mortality data using a stacked bar chart. This is to showcase trends in deaths causes by a varity of different causes of mortality over time, grouped by decades. It allows users to interactively explore the causes of death by selecting specific gender, countries and causes, filtering the data dynamically.

The visualisation features colour-coded bars, where each cause of death is represented by a specific color, making it easier to compare. A tooltip is also added to provide further detail about specific causes and their associated death rates when users hover over the bar. There is a slight transition that also occurs when users do hover over a bar.

Lastly, a legend table is provided to provide clarity to users for what each cause colour represents as well as enabling users to toggle causes on and off. This updates the chart dynamically and shows only the selected causes. The causes filter restarts when users choose a different country/sex.

This is useful for public health professional, researchers, policymakers or people interested in mortality who want to track how mortality patterns evolve, identify patterns, prominent causes of death in different countries, or evaluate the impact of health interventions over time. By visualisaing this data in a stacked bar chart, users can gain insights into trends quickly that would be difficult to see through raw datasets.

Muzner's guidelines and Tufte's design principles were taken into consideration when developing the stacked bar chart. The chart design emphiased on accuracy, clarity and effiency in portraying the data. Munzner's guideline focused on matching visual representation to the task, using appriopiate encodings, and balancing usability and complexity. While Tufte's principles of avoiding unncessary clutter, implementing data-ink maximisation and making sure that every visual element provides a purpose in communicating data insight. As a result of these principles, they help to create an informative and inuttive visualisaiton.

Data Sources and Transformation

All data were sourced from the OECD Health Statistics datasets. The Health status dataset includes data about Causes of mortality , which formed the foundation of our final data.
The unfiltered dataset contains ~952,000 data points, and was transformed in a relatively simple manner to reduce the overall size and bloat. As introduced in previous Data Science units, KNIME was utilised to smoothly mould our data to a more desirable form.
